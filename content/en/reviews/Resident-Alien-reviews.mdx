<Section icon={Swords} title="Comic vs. Show: Key Differences in Resident Alien">
While the *Resident Alien* TV series is based on the Dark Horse comic books, there are several notable differences that have shaped the show's unique identity. Understanding these distinctions can enhance appreciation for both adaptations and are frequently discussed in **Resident Alien reviews** comparing the two.

<Callout type="warning" title="Expect Divergences">
Fans of the original *Resident Alien* comic series should approach the TV show with an open mind, as significant plot points, character motivations, and overall tone have been adapted to suit the television format.
</Callout>

Here's a comparison of some major differences:

| Feature | Comic Book Version | TV Show Version | Impact on Narrative |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Harry's Mission** | Sent to recover a crashed probe to prevent humans from reverse-engineering alien tech. | Sent to Earth with a device to destroy all human life. | Sets up a moral dilemma for Harry as he grows to care for humans. |
| **Harry's Appearance** | Projects a psychic illusion of human form; is actually a purple, hairless humanoid. | Physically transforms into Harry Vanderspeigle after murdering him. | The show's physical transformation allows for more visual comedy and immediate danger from Max. |
| **Doctor's Murder** | Original doctor (Sam) murdered by someone from his past for personal reasons; Harry's psychic powers help solve it. | Original doctor poisoned, with Harry taking his place after the murder. | The show's murder mystery is more central to Harry's initial integration into Patience. |
| **Tone** | Primarily a mystery with dry humor; Harry is a smooth, analytical investigator. | A vibrant blend of comedy, drama, and mystery; Harry is awkward and learning human behavior. | The TV show leans heavily into the "fish out of water" comedy, making Harry's interactions more overtly humorous. |
| **Max's Ability** | One in a million humans can see Harry's true form due to a specific gene. | Max, a child, can see Harry's true form, leading to an ongoing antagonistic yet endearing dynamic. | Max's role is significantly expanded in the show, becoming a key antagonist and later ally to Harry. |

The decision to make Harry's mission one of planetary destruction in the show adds a layer of dramatic tension and moral complexity that is less prominent in the comics. This shift allows for Harry's character arc to be more profound as he grapples with the implications of his original directive versus his burgeoning humanity.

</Section>
